Design Philosophy

Constraints are catalysts.

The best design often emerges from real-world limitations: bandwidth, literacy levels, cultural context. I design for the edges, and the centre follows.

Research before rectangles.

I won't open Figma before I've spoken to real users. Every pixel should trace back to a human insight, not an assumption.

Systems over screens.

Great products are ecosystems, not interfaces. I think in flows, states, and design languages, not just single screens.

Motion has meaning.

Animation isn't decoration. It's information, it tells you what happened, what will happen, and why something matters.

UniDash, Unified Student Dashboard

Kenyan university students were navigating many apps for basic academic tasks. UniDash unified class timetables, projects, bootcamps, internships and daily personal tasks into a single accessible platform helping students meet their deadlines.

Role
Lead Product Designer
Duration
4 months · 2026
Tools
Figma
Platform
Mobile (Android-first)

Problem

University students, especially those in technology programs, often struggle to manage their academic schedules, projects, internships, and personal study activities due to the increasing complexity of their workloads. Most existing task management tools are generic and do not accommodate the structured nature of university semesters, class timetables, and project-based learning. As a result, students rely on fragmented tools or manual planning methods, which leads to disorganization, missed deadlines, and inefficient time management. This creates a need for a specialized academic task management system that can organize and schedule student workloads in a structured and realistic manner.

Research

To understand the specific challenges faced by technology university students in managing academic workloads, a survey-based research study was conducted. The goal was to identify: Existing habits in task management Problems with current digital and manual planning tools Preferences for features in a student-focused task tracker How academic, project, and internship workloads affect time management

Design Process

5 rounds of low fidelity to high fidelity prototyping were done, this is to ensure that all the user requirements we meet and the proposed solution is within the reqired scope and budget.

Solution

UniDash, a structured academic task management system designed specifically for university students. The system will allow students to organize their academic schedules, including class timetables, projects, internships, exams, and personal study sessions, within a single platform. UniDash will use a rule-based scheduling engine to automatically break down complex tasks and allocate them into realistic time slots while respecting fixed commitments such as lectures and work hours. By providing a centralized and structured planning environment, the system aims to improve time management, reduce missed deadlines, and help students manage their academic workloads more effectively.

Accessibility

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance, high contrast mode, screen reader support. Designed for 2G networks with progressive loading and skeleton states.

MawinguOps, Farmers Advisory Dashboard

Smallholder maize farmers in Kenya face frequent crop failures due to increasingly unpredictable weather patterns caused by climate change. Although weather forecasts exist, they are often too technical and generalized for farmers to translate into practical planting decisions. Most farmers rely on unreliable sources such as neighbors or radio broadcasts when deciding when to plant. This information gap leads to poor planting timing, reduced yields, and increased food insecurity.

Role
Operations Lead
Duration
5 months
Tools
Google Gemini AI, Google Cloud Run, Node.js, Express.js, Africa’s Talking API, SQLite, Weather APIs, AI model APIs
Platform
Web (Desktop-first)

Problem

1. Climate variability disrupting farming Weather patterns in Kenya have become unpredictable due to climate change. Traditional planting calendars that farmers relied on for generations no longer work. 2. High crop failure among smallholder farmers Over 80% of smallholder farmers in semi-arid regions experience crop losses. Incorrect planting timing leads to reduced maize yields and food insecurity. 3. Weather information is difficult to interpret Available forecasts are technical and complex (probabilities, pressure, rainfall mm). Farmers need simple planting decisions, not scientific weather reports. 4. Poor delivery of agricultural advisory information Existing channels have major limitations: Government extension officers cannot reach thousands of farmers quickly. Digital advisory apps require smartphones and internet. Radio forecasts are general and unreliable at farm level. 5. Accessibility barriers Most farmers: Use basic feature phones Have limited internet access Need solutions compatible with USSD or SMS.

Design Decisions

To address those problems, the team made several key system design decisions.1. Use simple mobile channels (USSD + SMS). Deliver advisories through USSD and SMS 2. Translate weather data into simple decisions. Use an AI advisory engine to convert weather forecasts into clear recommendations. 3. Use cloud-native serverless infrastructure. Deploy backend on serverless cloud infrastructure. 4. Provide hyper-local recommendations. Process location-specific weather forecasts. 5. Use a freemium business model. Basic advisory service remains free.Premium features available for advanced insights

Outcome

The solution aims to achieve several outcomes. 1. Improved planting decisions. 2. Increased maize yield. 3. Reduced crop failure. 4. Improved food security. 5. Economic impact

Healthcare Stress, UX Research Study

Healthcare professionals often experience high levels of stress after completing shifts due to long hours, night work, and exposure to trauma, which can impair recovery, sleep, and overall well-being. Despite the availability of technological tools like wearable health trackers and stress-monitoring apps, their use in managing post-shift stress remains underexplored. Traditional stress management strategies may not address real-time monitoring or personalized interventions, highlighting a critical gap in supporting healthcare workers’ post-shift recovery. This study seeks to investigate this issue and evaluate how technology can effectively reduce post-shift stress in clinical settings.

Role
Lead UX Researcher
Duration
3 months
Methods
Interviews, Surveys, Diary Studies
Participants
nurses & doctors, hospitals

Research Questions

What is the nature and extent of post-shift stress experienced by medical workers across different healthcare settings? Which occupational and environmental factors most significantly contribute to post-shift stress among healthcare professionals? How does post-shift stress affect the physical, psychological, and professional well-being of medical workers? What technological tools (e.g., wearable devices, stress-monitoring applications, digital health platforms) are currently available to detect and manage post-shift stress? How feasible and acceptable is the integration of technology-based stress management solutions in healthcare work environments? What evidence-based recommendations can be made for implementing technological interventions to reduce post-shift stress and support recovery?

Key Findings

Medical workers experience significant post-shift stress, manifested through fatigue, emotional exhaustion, and disrupted sleep, which negatively affects both their well-being and patient care. Factors such as long hours, night shifts, high patient acuity, and exposure to trauma were identified as major contributors. Technological tools like wearable devices and stress-monitoring apps show potential in tracking stress in real time, but adoption is limited due to usability, privacy, and workflow integration challenges.

Recommendations

Healthcare institutions should implement technology-driven stress management solutions tailored to post-shift recovery, including wearable monitors and digital health platforms. Training and awareness programs can improve adoption and proper usage of these tools. Policies should address data privacy, usability, and integration with existing workflows to ensure sustainable support. Finally, evidence-based interventions should be incorporated into occupational health strategies to enhance staff resilience and improve patient care outcomes.
